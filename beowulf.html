Themes of Beowulf

Comitatus

The relationship between kinsman, in which warriors known as Thanes pledged their loyalty toa lord. The lord provided riches, weapons, and land, in exchange for the Thanes' protection and vengeance upon death. This is an important idea in Germanic culture.

Glory and Pride

As Beowulf states, the only thing that survives death is glory. Warriors often boast about previous accomplishments and familial lineage, signifying superiority. However, too much pride can lead to selfishness. One example of this is Heremod, a Danish king who succumbed to pride and became very greedy and disliked.

Christianity vs. Paganism

Beowulf is an oral poem, which has been passed on through the ages despite shifts in religion. While Pagan references are often made, such as in Pagan funerals, the Christian God is frequently mentioned with wyrd, or fate. It's possible that the Christian scribes, who wrote the first manuscript of Beowulf, included their own Christian ideas in the poem.

Summary of Beowulf

Beowulf, along with 14 other warriors, sails to Denmark upon hearing that Hrothgar, king of the Danes, was suffering from attacks by a man-monster named Grendel. Beowulf is treated rather well upon arrival, enjoying a feast at Heorot. Unferth, son of Ecglaf, taunts him by mentioning his defeat in a swimming match against Breca as a youth. Beowulf stays overnight at Heorot, and is able to defeat Grendel with his bare hands. Beowulf rips Grendel's arm off and Grendel returns to the fen and dies. Grendel's mother wants vengeance, and kills Hrothgar's right hand man, Aeschere. Beowulf and his men follor her to her fen, and Beowulf uses an old sword from the giants that he found in Grendel's mother's home killing her with it. Beowulf is rewarded with riches and friendship from both king Hrothgar and king Hygelac (when he returns to the Geats).
Beowulf eventually becomes king of the Geats, when king Hygelac is killed. He successfully rules for half a century. However, a slave running from his master steals a cup from a treasure-guarding dragon, which angers the dragon and burns down the Geat civilization. Beowulf forces the slave to show him and ten of Beowulf's Thanes where the dragon lives, and Beowulf attempts to slay it. Only Wiglaf, out of all the warriors, aids Beowulf in killing the dragon. Beowulf also dies from the wounds, and is given a Pagan funeral at the end of the poem.

Commentary of Beowulf

Beowulf is an oral poem, told by Anglo-Saxon scops, with its first manuscript dating back to the 11th century. The manuscript, written by two Christian scribes, most likely incorporated Christian elements into the otherwise Pagan story. The story of Beowulf includes the characteristics and culture that existed in Germanic society. Certain qualities, like bravery and glory, are exhibited in its importance throughout the poem. Beowulf includes literary elements like alliteration, kennings, and caesuras, while providing a starting point for English literature in the centuries to come.

Characters

Beowulf
Son of Ecgtheow

Beowulf is the protagonist, who is a thane of Geat king Hygelac. When Hygelac is killed, Beowulf becomes king of the Geats due to his past accomplishments including ridding the Danes of Grendel. Beowulf also defeats Grendel's mother and the dragon, with the help of Wiglaf.

Hrothgar
King of the Danes

Hrothgar is the king of the Danes, who builds the magnificent hall known as Heorot. He is the father of Hrethric, Hrothmund, and Freawaru. Hrothgar accepts the help of Beowulf when Grendel terrorizes Heorot.

Wiglaf
Son of Weohstan

Wiglaf, the son of Weohstan, is one of Beowulf's most loyal Thanes. When the other Thanes choose to flee in the battle against the dragon, Wiglaf is the only warrior who aids Beowulf in defeating it. Wiglaf eventually becomes the king of the Geats when Beowulf dies.

Grendel
Descendent of Cain

Grendel, a descendent of Cain, is a man-monster who eats men. He begins to hate the Danes, having to listen to their celebrations constantly. Grendel attacks and kills many of the Danes, forcing King Hrothgar in a tough situation where he has to close Heorot. Grendel is eventually slayed by Beowulf, who pulls the arm off his socket.

Grendel's Mother
Descendent of Cain

Grendel's mother, also a descendent of Cain, is the mother of Grendel. When Grendel dies, she comes to Heorot and murders Aeschere, in revenge for Grendel's death. Beowulf eventually severs her neck and kills her in her fen.

Dragon
Old Harrower of the Dark

The greedy, fire-breathing dragon guards a barrow of golden treasure which an old tribe had left behind. A slave, running away from his abusive master, steals a cup from the hoard which awakens and angers the dragon. The dragon destroys the Geat towns, forcing Beowulf to kill it in the barrow. Both characters die in this encounter.

Other Characters

Wealtheow

Hrothgar's wife and queen of the Danes. She presents necklaces and mead-cups at Heorot.



Hrethric & Hrothmund

The sons of Hrothgar. Hrethric eventually inherits the throne after Hrothgar's death, but is killed when Hrothulf, Hrethric's cousin, betrays him.



Aeschere

Hrothgar's right hand man, who gets slain by Grendel's mother. The search party finds his head hanging on the cliffside.



Sigemund

An old hero that Beowulf is compared to, who is known for slaying a dragon.



Unferth

The son of Ecglaf, who killed his brother. He is jealous of Beowulf at the beginning of the poem, talking of his flaws. Later on, he becomes more generous and lends Beowulf his sword, Hrunting.



Hygelac

The king of the Geats and husband to Hygd. He gives Beowulf riches and land upon Beowulf's victories against Grendel and his mother.



Breca

Beowulf's rival who competes with Beowulf in a swimming match at a young age. Unferth brings him up while taunting Beowulf.



Wulfgar

Hrothgar's herald and messenger. He provides information to king Hrothgar upon Beowulf's arrival.



Literary Devices in Beowulf:

Alliteration

The repetition of an initial sound in neighboring words of a poem.

"They had seen me boltered in the blood of enemies
When I battled and bound five beasts"
lines 419-420

Consonance

The repetition of a consonant sound anywhere within the neighboring words of a poem.

"No counsel could ever expect
Fair reperation from those rabid hands"
lines 157-158


Assonance

The repetition of a vowel sound anywhere within the neighboring words of a poem.

"You waded in, embracing water,
taking its measure, mastering
currents, riding on the swell."
lines 513-515


Caesura

A gap in a line of poetry that indicates a rhythmic pause.

In Anglo-Saxon poetry, 2 accents fall on the left of the caesura and one on the right.
Oft Scyld Scefing | sceapena preatum,
monegum maegpum | meodo-setla ofteah;
egsode Eorle, | syddan aerest weard


Kenning

Metaphorical circumlocations (lengthy way of expressing something), signifying a person or thing by a characteristic or quality.

Hellish fiend = Grendel
Spearbearers = Warriors
Beowulf = "Bee-hunter"
Wealtheow = "Peace servant"


Epithet

An adjective of descriptive phrase expressing a quality characteristic of the person or thing mentioned.

The Eternal Lord = God
Sin-stained demon = Grendel
